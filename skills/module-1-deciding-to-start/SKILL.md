# YC Startup School — Module 1: Deciding to Start

This module addresses the most fundamental question every aspiring founder faces: should you actually start a startup? Harj Taggar (YC Group Partner) breaks down the decision framework, what traits actually predict success, and how to prepare if you're not ready yet.

## Instructions

1. Read the transcript from `${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/transcripts/startup-school/module-1/should-you-start-a-startup.md` using the Read tool
2. Present the key frameworks and mental models from the lecture with speaker attribution (Harj Taggar, YC Group Partner)
3. Analyze the user's current situation:
   - Ask about their current job/context if not known
   - Apply the worst-case analysis framework to their specific situation
   - Assess their resilience signals based on what they share
4. Generate a workbook-style self-assessment with questions derived from the lecture
5. Save the completed assessment to `knowledge/yc-startup-school/module-1-assessment.md` in the user's project root

## Key Topics

- **Resilience over credentials**: Why traditional success signals (school, big company) are weak predictors of founder success
- **Benchling case study**: Sajith's story — quiet engineer → $6B company CEO through sheer resilience
- **Worst-case analysis**: Practical framework — "What do I have to lose?" as a real calculation, not rhetoric
- **Learning as hidden asset**: Startup experience has career value even if the startup fails (Rippling hires 50+ former founders)
- **Initial motivations don't matter**: Money, curiosity, passion — all valid starting points; what matters is enduring motivation
- **Preparation path**: How to find co-founders through side projects and smart people conversations
- **The "few people really love" signal**: Paul Buchheit's heuristic — 1 passionate user > 1M indifferent signups
- **When to leap**: Energy differential between day job and side projects as the key indicator

## Output Format

### Part 1: Module Summary
- Core insight: resilience is the #1 predictor, not brilliance or confidence
- The worst-case framework with real numbers (typical: 1 year, below-market salary)
- Key frameworks: initial motivation → enduring motivation, learning asset value
- Memorable quotes and examples from the transcript

### Part 2: Apply to Your Situation
- Worst-case scenario analysis specific to the user's context (age, job, obligations)
- Signals of resilience the user has already demonstrated
- Current environment assessment: are they around potential co-founders?
- Energy differential check: draining job vs. energizing side work?

### Part 3: Self-Assessment Workbook
Generate 5-8 questions drawn directly from the lecture:

1. What is the worst-case scenario if you started a startup today — and can you genuinely live with it?
2. What would you be doing between conversations to learn more about the problem space you care about?
3. Who are the 2-3 smartest people you know that you most enjoy talking about ideas with?
4. Have you ever pushed through significant rejection or failure? What did that experience reveal about you?
5. Do you have a side project or idea right now? If someone used it and said "this changed how I do X" — what would that mean to you?
6. What's the energy differential between your current work and the things you work on voluntarily?
7. If you didn't start now, what would need to be true in 1 year for you to feel ready?
